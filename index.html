Abstraction

Within the world of Virtual Reality, spatial audio is often used as a supplement to visual cues to promote presence and immersion. Virtual reality aims to transport you into another world through carefully crafted graphics, rendering methods, physics, and many other elements that makes the experience feel realistic. But what happens in virtual environments with low visibility? Take video games for example. How can developers create that same sense of immersion and presence when are they limited by what the player can see? This project explores that question by exploring whether spatial audio alone can support navigation in a low visibility virtual environment, without relying on any visual cues to guide the user.

Background

Humans naturally use sound and spatial awareness in their day to day lives. As one of our main five senses, sound is a major way that we understand our environment, navigate through it, and even protect ourselves. This is not to negate the importance of our other senses, but rather to acknowledge the significant role that audio plays in working alongside them to help us make sense of the world around us. The main goal of VR systems is presence and immersion, making you feel like you are truly somewhere else. However, because vision is the dominant sense, most VR research and development has focused heavily on visual cues compared to audio.

Summary

This project was developed using Unity as the primary game engine, the XR Interaction Toolkit for controller use and locomotion, and Meta Quest 3 headset with a USB-C adaptor. The audio assets used in the project were sourced from freesount.org. Lastly, version control was managed through GitHub.

These were some of the main components of the project. The first step was setting up the Meta Quest 3 headset and ensuring the controllers were properly configured and functioning within Unity. This required installing the XR Interaction Toolkit, adding the correct controller interaction profiles, and importing the Starter Assets prefab to get everything working properly.

For the maze structure, 3D objects within Unity were used with colliders placed on the walls to prevent the user from walking through them. The main directional light was dimmed significantly to limit visibility and create a dark atmosphere. Although the environment was darker, further improvements to the visuals and overall quality of the environment are planned for future iterations.

Spatial audio was set up at the exit of the maze to guide users toward the exit using distance attenuation — the further away you are from the exit, the quieter the sound becomes. Lastly, a timer was added to the player's view to track how long it takes the user to complete the maze. When the player reaches the exit the timer stops and the controllers deliver a haptic pulse as a physical confirmation of completion.

Future Implementations

Gamification

  • Add multiple levels with increasing maze complexity
  • Increase difficulty by making the spatial audio quieter each level
  • Add a timer to track how long it takes to complete the maze
  • Add a leaderboard so players can compare and compete on their times

Darker Environment

  • Further reduce visibility inside the maze to increase reliance on spatial audio
  • Improve overall visual quality and atmosphere of the maze environment
  • Add more detailed textures to the walls, floor, and ceiling

Data Collection & Real World Impact

  • Collect data from users including completion times, paths taken, and hesitation points
  • Use this data to measure how effectively spatial audio supports navigation
  • Contribute findings to broader research on audio based navigation in XR environments
  • Apply insights to real world applications such as accessibility tools for visually impaired users
  • Help developers build more inclusive and immersive experiences across other devices and platforms
